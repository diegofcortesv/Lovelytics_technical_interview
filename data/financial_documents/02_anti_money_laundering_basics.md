# Anti-Money Laundering (AML) Basics

## Introduction
Anti-Money Laundering (AML) refers to the laws, regulations, and procedures designed to prevent criminals from disguising illegally obtained funds as legitimate income.

## The Three Stages of Money Laundering

### 1. Placement
- **Definition**: Introducing illicit funds into the financial system
- **Methods**: 
  - Structuring (smurfing): Breaking large sums into smaller deposits
  - Currency smuggling across borders
  - Purchasing high-value items (art, jewelry, real estate)
  - Casino gambling with cash

### 2. Layering
- **Definition**: Separating illegal proceeds from their source through complex transactions
- **Methods**:
  - Multiple wire transfers between accounts
  - Converting funds to different currencies
  - Purchasing and selling investments
  - Using shell companies and trusts
  - Cross-border transfers through multiple jurisdictions

### 3. Integration
- **Definition**: Reintroducing laundered money into the economy as legitimate funds
- **Methods**:
  - Real estate investments
  - Luxury purchases
  - Business investments
  - False invoicing in international trade

## Key AML Regulations

### Bank Secrecy Act (BSA)
- Requires financial institutions to report suspicious activities
- Mandates record-keeping of cash transactions
- CTR (Currency Transaction Report) for transactions > $10,000

### USA PATRIOT Act
- Enhanced due diligence requirements
- Prohibits anonymous accounts
- Increased information sharing between agencies

### Financial Action Task Force (FATF)
- International standards for AML/CFT
- 40 Recommendations for combating money laundering
- Mutual evaluation of member countries

## AML Program Components

### 1. Customer Due Diligence (CDD)
- Identity verification
- Understanding customer business and transaction patterns
- Ongoing monitoring of customer relationships

### 2. Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)
- Required for high-risk customers
- Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs)
- Customers from high-risk jurisdictions
- Complex business structures

### 3. Suspicious Activity Reporting (SAR)
- Filed when transactions raise red flags
- Must include detailed narrative
- Protected by safe harbor provisions
- Should not be disclosed to the customer

### 4. Transaction Monitoring
- Automated systems to detect unusual patterns
- Real-time or near-real-time analysis
- Rule-based and AI/ML approaches
- Alert generation and investigation

## Red Flags for Money Laundering

### Transaction Patterns
- Large cash deposits inconsistent with business type
- Rapid movement of funds through accounts
- Transactions just below reporting thresholds
- Multiple accounts with transfers between them

### Customer Behavior
- Reluctance to provide information
- Providing false or suspicious information
- Unusual concern about reporting requirements
- Little concern for fees or investment performance

### Business Activities
- Business generates excessive cash
- No clear business purpose
- Complex ownership structures
- Inconsistent with stated business activity

## Know Your Customer (KYC)

### Essential Information
- Full legal name and any aliases
- Date of birth and nationality
- Residential/business address
- Identification documents (passport, driver's license)
- Source of funds and wealth

### Ongoing Monitoring
- Regular updates to customer information
- Transaction monitoring for changes in patterns
- Periodic review of high-risk accounts
- Enhanced monitoring for PEPs

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

### For Institutions
- Hefty fines (millions to billions of dollars)
- Regulatory sanctions and restrictions
- Reputational damage
- Loss of banking licenses

### For Individuals
- Criminal prosecution
- Imprisonment (up to 20 years)
- Personal fines
- Professional disbarment

## Best Practices

1. **Risk-based approach**: Allocate resources based on risk assessment
2. **Strong compliance culture**: Leadership commitment to AML
3. **Regular training**: Keep staff updated on latest schemes
4. **Technology investment**: Modern monitoring and analytics tools
5. **Independent testing**: Regular audits of AML programs
6. **Information sharing**: Collaborate with law enforcement and peers
